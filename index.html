What is Quantum Computing?

A Basic Overview

Quantum computing is a developing form of technology that was first proposed in the 1980s. At present, quantum computing still has a ways to go before unlocking its full potential. The goal of this technology is to create models for systems that seem simple, but normal computers do not have the computing power to address due to complex factors such as the interactions of particles.

This technology will be especially useful for mapping every possible outcome of a system. While at the surface level, this seems like a fairly easy task that can be solved with simple combinations equations, the problems become increasingly complex as the number of factors to account for increases. Additionally, there is the possibility that these different configurations of a system interfere with each other.

The function of a quantum computer is to figure out all of these possible scenarios or quantized systems, creating what is called the quantum state. Microsoft explains, “the foundational core of quantum computing is to store information in quantum states of matter and to use quantum gate operations to compute on that information, by harnessing and learning to ‘program’ quantum interference.” A series of quantum gate operations creates a quantum algorithm that can be used to better understand quantum interference. Quantum interference is one of the foundation principles of quantum theory and essentially argues that an elementary particle can only be in one place at a time and therefore can not interfere with its own trajectory. However, individual particles can interfere with their own course of trajectory, thus altering the particle's course.

Essentially, quantum computers are not designed to solve equations and create models faster than a regular or super computer, but to solve a completely different type of problem. These problems are called bounded-error quantum problems or BQPs.
